\subsection{Background \& Motivation}

% NOTE: Should I explain that moderns software testing approaches do not apply to DNN verifications sf
AI, particularly deep neural networks (DNNs), has gained considerable traction in recent years. Consequently, their use in high-risk domains has increased. However, it has become evident that DNNs are not free from potential errors, such as exhibiting erroneous behaviour in response to trivial perturbations in the input \cite{intriguing_properties_of_nn}. Therefore, ensuring the robustness of DNNs rigorously is essential.


DNN verifiers address the challenging task of ensuring that a neural network satisfies specific safety, robustness, or fairness properties. However, these verifiers are not free from implementation bugs. Furthermore, most state-of-the-art DNN verifiers rely on floating-point arithmetic, which results in serious scalability concerns due to numerical instability \cite{proof_minimization_in_nn_verification}. These concerns can be exploited in order to compromise a fully verified neural network \cite{exploiting_verified_nn}, thereby defeating the purpose of DNN verifiers by raising unsoundness issues.

To ensure the correctness of a DNN verifier, one could consider verifying it by formalising and then proving its implementation. Nevertheless, since DNN verifiers are very complex programs that can even rely on multiple external software components, classical program verification techniques are not feasible. For that reason, the idea of \textit{Self-Certification} \cite{program_correctness} was introduced. \textit{Self-Certification} introduces a lightweight alternative to correctness by generating and checking certificates for every program execution, rather than verifying the program as a whole.


The software responsible for generating and verifying the certifications is a \textit{proof checker}. The exact function of the proof checker within the DNN verification pipeline is illustrated in \autoref{fig:dnn_verification_pipeline}. In Marabou, the standard \textit{proof checker} does not provide any formal guarantee of its own correctness. For that reason, a fully reliable implementation of Marabou's proof checker was developed in Imandra \cite{certified_proof_checker}, an industrial interactive theorem prover (ITP). Imandra does not suffer from floating-point imprecision since it supports arbitrary-precision real arithmetic, which guarantees numerical soundness.


Despite the success of implementing a fully correct proof checker for Marabou in Imandra, its adoption and maturity within the current theorem proving landscape are less significant than those of other ITPs, such as Rocq (formerly known as Coq), Lean, or Isabelle \cite{survey_coq_users}. This ultimately limits the reach and impact of these developments. Furthermore, Imandra's underdeveloped ecosystem can lead to increased code complexity and reduced maintainability compared to an equivalent implementation in a well-established ITP with a richer ecosystem.

\subsection{Aim and Objectives}


The role that the proof checker takes in a DNN verification pipeline is shown in \autoref{fig:dnn_verification_pipeline}. The proof checker verifies the correctness of the certificates generated by the verifier when no unsafe behaviour is found, meaning that the verifier was unable to find any counterexample witnessing an erroneous behaviour of the DNN.

The primary objective of this project is to translate and, if possible, extend the implementation of the Marabou \textit{proof checker} in Imandra by Desmartin et al. \cite{certified_proof_checker} into Rocq, an ITP and dependently typed programming language. The aim behind such translation has two main ideas. First, Rocq is significantly more widely adopted than Imandra, with a large community, a rich ecosystem of libraries, and an extensive history of academic work, which should make of implementing a certified proof checker easier. Second, by translating the certified proof checker into Rocq, we hope to increase attention to certified DNN verification and, hopefully, influence future developments along the same path.

The objectives of the proposed project are summarised in the following list:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Translating the proof checker.} Implement the proof checker in Rocq, ensuring that the resulting implementation is faithful to the original in terms of system-wise capabilities and overall structure.

	\item \textbf{Ensuring implementation soundness.} Verifying that the proof checker implemented in Rocq is sound and that it produces the same results as the Imandra implementation.

	\item \textbf{Evaluating the impact.} Compare the trade-offs between both implementations.
\end{itemize}
